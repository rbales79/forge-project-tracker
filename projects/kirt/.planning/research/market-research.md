> Researched: March 2026. Internal deployment context — not a SaaS launch analysis.
> Tool: ECC market-research (web search, 20+ primary sources). Full source list at end.

## Overview

KIRT operates at an intersection that no single incumbent owns. The market is fragmented across five overlapping categories — revenue intelligence, sales enablement, account intelligence data providers, meeting AI, and CRM extensions — but none of them solve KIRT's core problem: **unifying proprietary internal documents, CRM data, and public data into structured intelligence and finished deliverables for strategic account teams**.

The AI sales market is massive and accelerating. Global AI in sales reached an estimated $8.8B in 2025, growing 32%+ annually toward $63.5B by 2032. Sales intelligence specifically is $4.5B in 2026 with 10%+ CAGR through 2035. Large enterprises will capture 70% of that market by 2035 — the segment KIRT serves.

**The white space is real.** Every major platform is CRM-signal-first, call-transcript-centric, or data-enrichment-focused. None of them read your SharePoint, understand your internal delivery artifacts, or write finished client-facing documents back to your knowledge base. That is KIRT's lane.

**Gartner published its first-ever Magic Quadrant for Revenue Action Orchestration in December 2025**, formalizing a new category that converges sales engagement, conversation intelligence, and revenue intelligence. KIRT can position as the enterprise internal intelligence layer that feeds and extends this category without competing in it.

### Category 1: Revenue Intelligence — Gong, Clari/Salesloft, People.ai

**Gong**

Gong is the category leader with 27% market share and $300M+ ARR as of January 2025. Built on conversation intelligence — call recording, transcription, and analysis — Gong has expanded into pipeline management, forecasting, and AI agents that automate follow-ups and forecast corrections. Pricing moved to a three-part model in March 2025: $1,600/user/year for Foundations, plus a $50K annual platform fee, plus $15K–$65K implementation. Add-ons (Forecast: $700/user/yr, Engage: $800/user/yr) push total cost well past $2,400/user/year at scale.

What Gong does well: Conversation analytics and coaching are best-in-class. Pipeline visibility from call signals is genuinely useful for sales leaders. The new agent layer is real.

What Gong does not do: It cannot read a customer's SharePoint proposal archive. It cannot analyze an internal delivery assessment. It does not generate an EBR deck. It does not write anything back to SharePoint. Its intelligence is entirely interaction-signal-based — emails and calls. The account-level intelligence it surfaces is derivative of those signals, not a synthesis of all available knowledge.

**Clari / Salesloft**

Clari merged with Salesloft in 2024 and now positions as a "Revenue Orchestration" platform. Clari's core strength is pipeline forecasting and revenue governance. It is the enterprise standard for forecast accuracy. Salesloft adds sales engagement (sequences, cadences). The combined platform is comprehensive for pipeline management but offers nothing in the way of document intelligence or deliverable generation. Pricing is custom enterprise.

What Clari does well: Forecast accuracy. Pipeline inspection. Revenue governance for exec-level visibility. It knows if your deal is at risk before your rep does.

What Clari does not do: Cannot read internal documents. Cannot generate account briefs, engagement prep, or client deliverables. Cannot interact with SharePoint in any meaningful way.

**People.ai**

People.ai captures and correlates sales activity (emails, calls, calendars) with Salesforce opportunity data to surface insights about buying groups, engagement patterns, and deal risk. Recognized as a Visionary in Gartner's first Revenue Action Orchestration MQ (December 2025). Recently added MCP integration for accessing revenue intelligence via existing AI workflows (Claude, ChatGPT, Slack).

What People.ai does well: Activity attribution and correlation at enterprise scale. Buying group identification. CRM hygiene automation. The MCP integration is a meaningful architecture move.

What People.ai does not do: No document intelligence. No deliverable generation. Its intelligence layer is purely activity-signal-based. Internal knowledge (proposals, assessments, architecture docs) is invisible to it.

### Category 2: Sales Enablement — Seismic + Highspot, Showpad

**Seismic + Highspot (Merging)**

Seismic and Highspot announced a definitive merger agreement on February 12, 2026 — the two dominant sales enablement platforms are combining. The combined entity will operate as Seismic under CEO Rob Tarkoff. The merged platform will span content management, learning/coaching, analytics, and engagement, with Seismic's Aura AI and Highspot's Nexus AI engine forming the combined intelligence layer. Seismic's Winter 2026 release includes AI-generated meeting summaries, mutual action plans (MAPs) in Digital Sales Rooms, and AI-generated audio summaries of content.

What Seismic + Highspot does well: Content management and findability at scale. Rep training and coaching. Content performance analytics (what content actually closes deals). The merger creates a dominant, comprehensive platform for revenue team readiness.

What it does not do: Sales enablement platforms are about managing and surfacing sales collateral — they do not analyze customer-specific context and generate account-specific deliverables. A Seismic template is not a KIRT-generated EBR. Content is generic and managed; KIRT output is specific and generated. No SharePoint ingestion. No account-specific intelligence synthesis.

**Showpad**

Showpad (merged with Bigtincan in 2024) positions as a "Revenue Effectiveness Platform" targeting field selling teams. AI-powered search surfaces relevant content, AI-generated asset summaries reduce browsing time, and the platform tracks content engagement. Enterprise contracts run $60K–$100K/year.

What Showpad does well: Field sales content delivery. Buyer engagement tracking. Offline content access for field teams.

What it does not do: Same fundamental gap as Seismic — content management, not intelligence generation. No account-specific synthesis.

### Category 3: Account Intelligence Data Providers — ZoomInfo, Apollo, Demandbase, 6sense

**ZoomInfo**

The default B2B contact and company data provider. Firmographic data, technographic data, intent signals, and contact information. Enterprise pricing starts at $15,000+/year just for data access. The platform has been under pressure from AI-native alternatives that are faster to index and cheaper.

What ZoomInfo does well: Largest B2B contact database. Broad firmographic coverage. The default for outbound prospecting data.

What it does not do: Nothing about internal account knowledge. Cannot synthesize a customer's account history, prior engagements, or internal delivery artifacts. Public-data-only view of accounts.

**6sense**

Predictive account scoring and buyer intent identification. The value proposition is identifying which accounts are "in-market" before they raise their hand. Pricing: $50K–$100K+/year. Built for marketing and outbound, not account management of existing customers.

What 6sense does well: In-market account identification. ABM targeting and orchestration. Intent signal aggregation.

What it does not do: No value for existing customer intelligence. The entire platform is built for net-new pipeline. KIRT's primary context is strategic accounts that are already customers.

**Demandbase**

Similar to 6sense but adds native programmatic advertising. ABM/ABX orchestration for marketing teams targeting named accounts.

What it does not do: Same as 6sense — net-new outbound focus, no internal document intelligence, no deliverable generation.

**Apollo**

Self-serve prospecting intelligence. 275M+ contacts, $49–99/user/month. Strong for SMB and mid-market outbound. Less relevant to KIRT's enterprise strategic account context.

### Category 4: Meeting AI — Otter.ai, Fireflies.ai, Avoma

**Otter.ai**

Transcription, speaker identification, AI-generated meeting summaries. OtterPilot joins meetings automatically. Free to $30/user/month.

What Otter does well: Accurate transcription. Accessible price point. Good UX for broad organizational use.

What it does not do: Summaries are generic — not structured for account intelligence. No synthesis with account history, prior meetings, or CRM data. No deliverable generation. No SharePoint integration.

**Fireflies.ai**

Call recording, transcription, searchable meeting archive, CRM sync (Salesforce, HubSpot). 90%+ transcription accuracy. 69-language support. Free tier is the most generous in the category. The API enables custom downstream use — worth noting as a potential ingest source for KIRT.

What it does not do: Same gaps as Otter — meeting summaries are not account intelligence. No synthesis, no deliverable generation.

**Avoma**

Positioned between meeting AI and conversation intelligence. AI notetaking plus CRM auto-fill of structured fields (Next Step, Competition Mentioned, Sentiment). Most sophisticated meeting AI for sales contexts. $49–79/user/month.

What Avoma does well: CRM field auto-population from meetings. Conversation analytics. Coaching built in. Closest to KIRT's meeting processing workflow.

What it does not do: Avoma produces structured meeting data; it does not consume that data alongside account history, SharePoint docs, and public signals to produce an EBR or a whitespace analysis. It is an upstream component, not a competing solution.

### Category 5: CRM Extensions — Salesforce Agentforce, Microsoft Copilot for Sales

**Salesforce Agentforce (formerly Einstein Copilot)**

Agentforce pricing: approximately $125/user/month for an all-inclusive AI model. Generates call summaries, email drafts, opportunity briefings, and sales emails from CRM data. Deep Salesforce Data Cloud integration means it can synthesize from any first-party data in Salesforce, but the synthesis is CRM-bounded — what's in Salesforce is all it knows.

What Agentforce does well: Native CRM integration eliminates adoption friction. Personalized email generation is good. Works where reps already live (SFDC). The agent framework is maturing quickly.

What it does not do: Cannot read SharePoint documents. Cannot synthesize from external or unstructured internal knowledge. Deliverable generation is limited to email drafts and meeting summaries — not EBR decks, account briefs, or engagement prep packages in KIRT's sense.

**Microsoft Copilot for Sales**

$30/user/month on top of Microsoft 365. Generates meeting summaries and email drafts in Outlook and Teams, surfaces CRM context in those apps, and writes back limited summaries to Dynamics/Salesforce. SharePoint is the number-one grounding source for Microsoft 365 Copilot — this is the most direct overlap with KIRT's architecture.

What Copilot for Sales does well: Lives inside Outlook/Teams where many enterprise users work. SharePoint grounding is real and improving. The Microsoft 365 Copilot + SharePoint Agents architecture (announced March 2026) creates genuine agentic capabilities inside the M365 ecosystem.

What it does not do: Copilot for Sales is a general assistant, not a strategic account intelligence system. It does not produce account briefs, engagement prep packages, whitespace analyses, or EBR/QBR documents. It summarizes what happened in a meeting; it does not synthesize all available account context to produce a structured intelligence artifact. It is scoped to the individual user's work context, not a team-shared intelligence layer.

**The Microsoft 365 Copilot threat vector**: The SharePoint Agents framework (GA March 2026) enables organizations to build custom agents grounded in SharePoint content. This is the closest thing to a platform KIRT could be displaced by — if Microsoft deepens SharePoint Agents with Salesforce connectors, SEC filing ingestion, and deliverable generation templates, it becomes a legitimate competitor. **Monitor quarterly.** For now, Copilot/SharePoint Agents are general-purpose and require significant custom development to approach KIRT's intentional account intelligence design.

### Category 6: Key Account Management Platforms — DemandFarm, Altify

**DemandFarm**

Salesforce-native KAM platform. Digitizes account planning with org charts, whitespace grids, relationship mapping, and opportunity prioritization. 2025–2026 rollout of "Kampanion" adds agentic AI: auto-account research, predictive playbooks, and one-click action plans. **The closest existing product to KIRT's account intelligence functions.**

What DemandFarm does well: Structured account planning methodology baked into CRM. Whitespace analysis. Relationship maps.

What it does not do: Cannot read SharePoint documents. Account research comes from CRM data and public signals, not internal delivery artifacts. Deliverables are account plan structured data, not finished documents (briefs, EBRs, engagement prep packages). No multi-modal LLM backend. No hybrid vector + keyword search.

**Altify (Upland Software)**

SFDC-native account planning with AI-generated strategy playbooks. Acquired by Upland Software — innovation velocity is uncertain.

What it does not do: Same fundamental limitations as DemandFarm — CRM-bounded intelligence, no internal document ingestion, no deliverable generation at KIRT's scope.

## Build / Fork / Extend Recommendation

**Recommendation: Greenfield build on proven infrastructure components.**

No existing platform can be forked or extended to deliver KIRT's core capability. The closest candidates (DemandFarm, Altify) are Salesforce AppExchange products with closed architectures. Microsoft Copilot for Sales is the most dangerous adjacency but requires deep Microsoft partner access and is intentionally general-purpose.

The right approach is already validated by the KIRT spec: build the intelligence layer (KIRT) on top of Forge Foundation's established data platform.

| Layer | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Document ingestion | Forge Foundation `POST /v1/ingest/` — already built |
| Vector store | Weaviate — proven, open-source, Forge-native |
| LLM abstraction | Multi-provider (Gemini, Azure OpenAI, Bedrock) |
| Search | Hybrid BM25 + semantic — Weaviate native |
| Frontend | React/Vite/Tailwind/Shadcn — KIRT spec confirmed |
| Backend | Django (MCP-native) — KIRT spec confirmed |
| CRM | Salesforce read-only (CSV V1, direct API V4+) |
| SP write-back | Microsoft Graph API — available, documented |
| Public data | SEC EDGAR API, web enrichment — proven sources |

Worth evaluating as infrastructure components (not replacements): LangGraph (agent orchestration), Haystack (RAG pipeline), Unstructured.io (document parsing).

**Do not extend CRM vendors.** Salesforce Agentforce and Microsoft Copilot are consumption surfaces, not intelligence platforms.

**Do not fork DemandFarm or Altify.** Closed commercial products, SFDC-native, wrong architecture for KIRT's multi-source intelligence model.

## Differentiation Analysis

KIRT's differentiation is structural — it stems from what data it can access and what it produces, not feature checkbox differences.

### KIRT's Three Structural Moats

**1. Unstructured internal document intelligence**
Every competitor is blind to internal documents: proposals, SOWs, delivery assessments, architecture reviews, prior EBRs, stakeholder maps in SharePoint. KIRT ingests and indexes these as first-class data. This is KIRT's highest-value differentiator because competitors cannot replicate it without a fundamentally different data architecture — and they are not building toward it.

**2. Deliverable generation, not summaries**
The market produces meeting summaries and CRM field updates. KIRT produces finished, structured deliverables: Account Briefs, Engagement Prep packages, Meeting Summaries, Client Deliverables. These are consumption-ready artifacts, not raw signals. The distinction is the difference between a transcript and a briefing document.

**3. SharePoint as source and destination**
KIRT reads from SP (ingests documents as evidence) and writes back to SP (delivers finished artifacts). No competitor does this. Microsoft Copilot for Sales writes limited summaries to SP, but it is not reading internal account archives and producing structured intelligence from them.

### Additional Differentiators

**4. Multi-source synthesis**
KIRT fuses three evidence streams: internal SP documents, Salesforce CRM records, and public signals (SEC filings, news). No competitor fuses all three. Gong/Clari see interaction signals. ZoomInfo/6sense see public data. Agentforce sees CRM. KIRT sees all of them and synthesizes.

**5. Graceful degradation with transparency**
KIRT's data completeness indicator (percentage quality score, flagged gaps, suggested actions) is a design feature no competitor has. Users know what they're getting and what would improve it. This is an enterprise trust mechanism, not a cosmetic feature.

**6. Proprietary methodology encoding**
The SAF (Strategic Account Framework) is encoded into KIRT's intelligence model. Whitespace grids, maturity scoring, phase-gated generation, and progression tracking are built around an internal methodology. Generic platforms cannot replicate this without being rebuilt from scratch.

### Where Competitors Are Stronger

- **Gong/Avoma**: Real-time coaching during calls. KIRT processes transcripts post-meeting.
- **Seismic + Highspot (merged)**: Content performance analytics across a large rep population.
- **6sense**: Buyer intent signals for net-new pipeline.
- **Microsoft 365 Copilot + SharePoint Agents**: Platform-level investment from Microsoft. **Monitor quarterly.**

## Market Positioning Signals

### Market Timing (Favorable)

1. **Gartner formalized Revenue Action Orchestration in December 2025** — enterprise buyers now have budget and vocabulary for this investment class.
2. **Seismic + Highspot merger (February 2026)** creates 12–18 months of customer uncertainty in enablement. Enterprise accounts reviewing their stack are evaluating alternatives.
3. **Microsoft SharePoint Agents (March 2026)** signals Microsoft's intent to own enterprise knowledge management AI — validates the category, accelerates enterprise readiness, without delivering what KIRT delivers.
4. **AI sales market CAGR 32%+** — budget is flowing to this problem.

### Internal Deployment Positioning

For an internal enterprise deployment, KIRT competes against:
- **Status quo**: Manual research, tribal knowledge, inconsistent prep
- **Generic AI tools**: ChatGPT/Copilot prompts without account context
- **Patchwork SaaS**: Gong + ZoomInfo + Seismic + DemandFarm (4 vendors, 4 contracts, no integration)

The ROI case is measured against the status quo, not against best-of-breed SaaS. The spec's stated target — 80%+ reduction in document prep time, 50–70% improvement in account intelligence quality — is achievable against that baseline.

### Positioning Statement (Internal)

> KIRT is the account intelligence layer that knows what your team knows — not just what your CRM records show. It reads your internal documents, synthesizes your account history, and produces deliverables your team actually uses. It runs on your infrastructure, writes to your SharePoint, and encodes your methodology.

### GTM Adoption Levers

1. **Day-one value per feature**: Each of KIRT's 5 V1 outputs delivers standalone value. An AE who saves 2 hours on meeting prep has already justified the tool.
2. **Where the workflow lives**: SP write-back and SF read make outputs part of existing workflows automatically.
3. **Exec-level visibility**: Sales Leadership as Consumer role is the internal champion signal.
4. **Low-friction beachhead**: On-demand AE use case (Meeting Summary in 10 minutes) is the fastest path to adoption. Win this first; SAF workflow follows.
5. **Measurement**: Instrument success metrics from Day 1 — time saved per deliverable, account coverage, deliverable quality scores, adoption by role.

### Risk Signals to Monitor

- **Microsoft SharePoint Agents convergence**: If Microsoft deepens Salesforce connector and adds account brief templates to SharePoint Agents, perceived differentiation narrows. KIRT's moat then depends on Foundation data quality, SAF methodology encoding, and LLM quality — all defensible. **Monitor quarterly.**
- **Gong/Clari adding document ingestion**: Either platform could add SP connectors. Timeline: 18–24 months before this is a mature feature.
